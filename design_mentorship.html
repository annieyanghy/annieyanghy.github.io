Mentorship app


A product concept to build a design community focused on mentorship.

Time

Sep. - Dec. 2019

Role

Solo Product Designer

Type

Solo class project

Methods

User Interview, Observation, Paper Prototyping, Questions Options and Criteria (QOC) Analysis, Hi-fi Mockup, Interactive Prototyping, Remote Usability Testing

Tools

Figma, Principle, Pen & paper, Procreate

Problem

Transitioning junior designers need mentorship but find it difficult or stressed to reach out to experienced professionals.

According to a survey by Indeed, 49% of employees have experienced a major career switch in their life, which also happens on the graduate students in the University of Michigan School of Information (UMSI). Thus, I want to know what made them stressful on the transitioning path.

1

Not knowing how to connect to experienced designers for advice.

2

Overwhelmed by the number of resources online.

3

Don't know where to get project opportunities.

Solution

An app with a 1-1 mentorship feature made for designers to enable conversations and share portfolios.

It's a friendly platform to connect professional UX designers to UX learners so that UX learners can feel comfortable to reach out to designers for guidance or work review.

    Research insight III

    Found a design opportunity: a friendly platform for newbie designers to build mentorship.


    I researched products mentioned by participants during the interviews, and found that none of these platforms provide a direct, supportive and personal environment for aspiring UX designers. Reaching out to professional designers on Linkedin -- can be very intimidating and scary for new UX learners when people display the most professional side of themselves on Linkedin.

    Mentorship

    Apprenticeship

    Friendly

    is so important for them to bond with others.

    1. Profile View

    The first iteration focuses on imitating the social media feature of swiping, which is a gesture associated more with making friends and 1-1 connection.

    The second iteration is based on users' feedback. In the second iteration, the design of the card pays more attention to the display of the background and interests of designers.

    2. Full-page Information

    First iteration: it's too focused on displaying all information, which, at the same time, render only one designer at a time.

    Second iteration: it renders a list of designers at a glance so that users can search for their ideal mentors more quickly.

    3. Share Portfolio

    The first iteration: focuses on the full-screen experience. However, this iteration also implies one risk: text legibility on different photos.

    Second iteration: the photo size is reduced, which allows more space for information display without risking the text readability.

    Future Task

    Continue testing

    Focus on sending portfolio and job searching based on users' feedback and market analysis.

    Interview mentors' side

    Previously, this app primarily focuses on the mentee's needs. In order to serve both mentee and mentors better, my next step will be to interview the mentor's side to find out what they would want from volunteering as a mentor.

    Defining success metrics

    Currently, the success metrics I set includes User Retention after 8 weeks, User Acquisition, NPR score, and New chats created between mentors and mentee.

    Reflective thinking in design

    During this design process, I grew in a supportive environment of encouraging reflection as a designer, which is a spirit seriously emphasized by our Professor Tawanna.     What is reflective design? I learned this concept in the Interaction Design class and totally think it represents my design philosophy because I think reflection on self-work is a crucial aspect of a UX designer. In the research paper Reflective design, Senders et al. mentioned, "We define 'reflection' as referring to critical reflection, or bringing unconscious aspects of experience to conscious awareness, thereby making them available for conscious choice." (Sengers et al., 2005)
